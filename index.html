Uncovering Personal Narratives from the Past: A Postcard from 1909
Transcription:

Mulberry, Fla. 4-4-9
Dear Papa just a few lines we are all well hope you all are well too. Sis & Pete have both settled. I was so glad to hear from you all tell her I will send you all each one a box of oranges the last of next week. There is a packing house here. She says her hand is most out of work she was going to send Papa a suit & my shoes to sell them. I will if they can come if they will pay my fare I will leave here for Orange about 22 you must come to Orange I can’t tell yet what day you can come but will let her know. Saturday I must tell Jessie I wrote her I could work again and am answering yours. Must write me before I leave here let me hear from you. Daughter.

The discovery of this postcard from 1909 provides a rare and intimate glimpse into the lives of an ordinary family in early 20th-century Florida. Penned from Mulberry, a small town in Polk County, Florida, the letter captures the daily realities and familial concerns of the time. Mulberry, known for its phosphate mining industry, was a burgeoning town, and the writer mentions a local packing house, likely for citrus fruits, a significant industry in Florida.

The daughter’s reference to sending a box of oranges highlights the agricultural backbone of the region. During this period, Polk County and nearby Orange County were prominent centers of citrus production. The mention of traveling to "Orange" likely refers to Orange County, emphasizing the interconnectedness of these agricultural communities. This simple act of sending oranges underscores the economic activities central to their livelihood.

The postcard itself, adorned with an image of a white rose and a poignant verse, adds a layer of emotional depth to the correspondence. The poem reads, "The rose that is sweetest and fairest Is the bud that is killed by the frost; And the love that is dearest and rarest, Is the true love that we have just lost." This poetic touch, combined with the rose's image, suggests a sentimental and perhaps melancholic tone. The choice of such a card could indicate the daughter's affection and care for her family, using a beautiful and meaningful image to convey her thoughts.

Considering the cost of postage and the postcard itself in 1909, this form of communication was relatively affordable. A postcard stamp cost only one cent, making it an accessible way for people to stay in touch across distances. This affordability ensured that even families of modest means could maintain connections, which was vital during an era when other forms of communication were limited and expensive.

Placing ourselves in the shoes of the daughter, we can imagine her daily life in Mulberry, balancing work, familial responsibilities, and the logistics of travel. Her writing conveys a sense of duty and affection, as she coordinates sending goods and plans her journey to Orange. For her father, Jasper, residing in Florence, South Carolina, receiving such a letter would have been a crucial lifeline, providing news and reassurance about the well-being of his family far away.

This postcard serves as a tangible link to the past, offering insights into the personal and economic dynamics of early 20th-century Florida. It reminds us of the resilience and resourcefulness of families during this period, as they navigated the challenges of their time with care and dedication. By examining such artifacts, we gain a deeper understanding of our shared history and the enduring human connections that transcend time.
